Neon Machine Inc. - Shrapnel

During my time at Neon Machine Inc., I worked as a Character Technical Artist on Shrapnel, a competitive, extraction-based first-person shooter developed in Unreal Engine 5. This near-future sci-fi title focuses on tactical resource extraction, high-stakes combat, and persistent progression. My responsibilities included collaborating with the Character Lead and Character team to achieve artistic and technical goals for the game’s characters. I managed and refined Metahuman-based character assets, developed cloth simulation and character customization systems, and contributed to R&D efforts. I also worked with the UI/UX team on rendering high-quality NFT weapon and character skins, defining best practices for real-time asset presentation within Unreal Engine. Additionally, I partnered with the Art Director on lighting setups and collaborated with the environment team to produce Nanite-based vehicles.

Shrapnel was my first professional video game project, giving me valuable insight into game production—how challenging it is to make a game and how rewarding it can be. I strengthened hard skills like weight painting and scripting, learned to efficiently manage thousands of character and clothing assets in-engine, and grew into a stronger Unreal technical artist overall. I also learned the importance of collaborating across teams and communicating effectively to ensure tasks were completed on time and at the highest quality.

One key learning experience involved converting an animated Nanite vehicle into a skeletal mesh due to runtime CPU costs. I also learned the technicalities of working with Metahuman assets, which, like Nanite, are not always optimal straight out of the box and require careful adjustment and tuning to perform well in a game environment. I walked away with new knowledge, such as how to begin using Unreal Insights for performance profiling and understanding that the Metahuman groom system is often the first area needing optimization. I do wish I had more time to dig further, as my time at Shrapnel ended sooner than expected, and I wish I had approached some areas with more urgency to learn even more while I had the chance. Even so, these experiences significantly deepened my understanding of performance optimization and cross-team problem-solving. While Shrapnel never reached a final release during my time there, it advanced to multiple early pre-beta stages and worldwide playtests—an invaluable experience that gave me a strong foundation in real-world game development, technical collaboration, and scalable asset workflows that I continue to build on today.
